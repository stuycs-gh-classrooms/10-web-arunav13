Stem Cell Potency

What is Cell Potency?

Cell Potency measures the ability of a stem cell to differentiate into other types of cells in the body. Below are different examples of cell potency:

  • Totipotency: Totipotent cells can differentiate into ANY specialized cell in the body, even extraembryonic cells! Fun fact: A totipotent cell can keep reproducing until it creates an entire organism.
  • Pluripotency: Pluripotent cells can differentiate into all the specialized cells in an adult organism, meaning they can’t give rise to placental/extraembryonic cells.
  • Multipotent: Multipotent cells give rise to multiple types of cells.
  • Unipotent: Unipotent cells can give rise to only one type of cell

Types of Potency

Cells have varying potencies depending on which state of human development they’re found in.

  • Totipotent Cells: The zygote is a totipotent cell, as it's the first cell in human development. The morula, a cluster of cells during early stages of embryonic development, also consists of totipotent cells. An example of a totipotent cell is a zygote.
  • Pluripotent Cells: From a morula, a blastocyst develops. The embryonic stem cells in a blastocyst are pluripotent.
  • Multipotent Cells: This includes germ layer stem cells.
    • Fun fact: In most vertebrates, stem cells that are pluripotent or totipotent are rare!

Embryonic Growth and Stem Cell Development

The three germ layers in later stages of embryonic development — ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm — all have stem cells dedicated to form a certain part of the body.

  • Ectoderm: Skin and Nervous system
  • Mesoderm: Skeletal and Muscular system
  • Endoderm: Lung, gut, and liver
  • Unipotent Cells: Somes examples include neural stem cells, which only give rise to neurons.
